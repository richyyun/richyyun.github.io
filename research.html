Cortical stimulation paired with volitional movement affects interhemispheric communication

Richy Yun, Andrew R. Bogaard, Andrew G. Richardson, Stavros Zanos, Steve I. Perlmutter, Eberhard E. Fetz

Keywords: cortical stimulation, voluntary movement, non-human primate, short-term plasticity

One of the most promising applications of neuroprosthetics is clinical rehabilitation. Stimulation techniques to induce plasticity and aid in the recovery of motor function following neurological damage from stroke or spinal cord injury have previously been implemented with varying degrees of success. To develop a more effective approach, we need a better understanding of how stimulation delivered during voluntary movements affects motor function. Although communication between the motor cortices of the two hemispheres is essential for coordinated behavior even during unilateral movements, previous studies that investigated the effects of delivering stimulation during movement typically focused on a single hemisphere and the contralateral limb. On the other hand, studies exploring the interplay between the two hemispheres typically delivered stimulation at rest. This study aims to bridge these approaches by comprehensively probing the effects of delivering stimulation to one motor cortex during different phases of voluntary contralateral or ipsilateral unimanual movement.

Embedding a panoramic representation of infrared light in the adult rat somatosensory cortex through a sensory neuroprosthesis

Konstantin Hartmann, Eric E. Thomson, Ivan Zea, Richy Yun, Peter Mullen, Jay Canarick, Albert Huh, Miguel A.L. Nicolelis

Keywords: sensory neuroprosthesis, rat barrel cortex

Understanding the potential for plasticity in the adult brain is a key goal for basic neuroscience and modern rehabilitative medicine. This study examines one dimension of this challenge: how malleable is sensory processing in adult mammals? We implemented a panoramic infrared (IR) sensory prosthetic system in rats; it consisted of four IR sensors equally spaced around the circumference of the head of the rat. Each sensor was coupled to a microstimulating electrode placed in the somatosensory cortex of the rat. Within days, rats learned to use the prosthesis to track down items associated with IR light in their environment. This is quite promising clinically, as the largest demand for sensory prosthetic devices is in adults whose brains are already fully developed.
